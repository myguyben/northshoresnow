A winter that goes smoothly was planned in the fall. The properties that struggle every
year aren't unlucky — they're improvising at 4 a.m. because nobody defined the triggers,
mapped the priorities, or agreed who gets the call. Use this checklist before the season to
turn [snow removal](/snow-removal) from a scramble into a system. It's written for property
managers and strata councils, and it works whether you self-perform or hire a contractor.

## 1. Walk the site before the snow

Do a physical pre-season walk in daylight, ideally with your contractor. You're looking for
the things a plow operator needs to know and the hazards that will bite in January:

- Where snow can — and can't — be stacked (watch sightlines, drains, and landscaping).
- Catch basins and drains that must stay clear so meltwater doesn't pond and refreeze.
- Low clearances, speed bumps, curbs, and islands that need marking before they're buried.
- Overnight-parked vehicles and access constraints that shrink your working window.
- Anything already damaged, photographed now so it isn't blamed on the plow later.

## 2. Map your priorities

Not everything gets cleared first, so decide the order *before* you need it. Rank zones by
traffic and risk:

1. **Life-safety and access first** — main entrances, accessible parking and ramps, fire
   lanes, the primary pedestrian routes.
2. **High-traffic circulation** — drive lanes, loading areas, secondary walkways.
3. **Everything else** — overflow parking, low-use corners.

Flag the **ice-prone micro-spots** specifically: north-facing walks that freeze first,
shaded stalls, ground below downspouts, and every slope or ramp. These get priority
[de-icing](/de-icing) and pre-treatment, because that's where falls actually happen. On the
North Vancouver hillside, freeze-thaw makes these spots a recurring
problem, not a one-off.

## 3. Define your triggers in writing

Vague triggers cause disputes at the worst possible moment. Nail down:

- **Snowfall trigger:** the accumulation depth that dispatches crews (commonly 2–3 cm).
- **Freezing trigger:** de-icing at **0°C** with moisture present — including clear days
  with no snow, when afternoon melt refreezes by evening. The
  [when-to-salt guide](/blog/when-to-salt-parking-lot) explains why this one prevents the
  most incidents.
- **Pre-treatment trigger:** anti-icing goes down *ahead* of a forecast storm or freeze.
- **Response time:** the clock from trigger to crews on-site, tiered to your opening hours.

## 4. Build the communication tree

When a storm hits, everyone should already know who calls whom. Write down:

- The single point of contact on your side, and their backup.
- The contractor's dispatch line and after-hours escalation.
- Who authorizes extra work (like snow hauling after an exceptional event) and up to what
  limit.
- How and when residents, tenants, or staff get notified — and what they're responsible for
  on their own limited common property.

One page, shared before the season, prevents the 5 a.m. round of "I thought *you* called
them."

## 5. Set your documentation standard

Documentation is both an operations tool and your
[liability defence](/blog/slip-and-fall-liability-commercial-property-bc). Decide up front
that every event will produce:

- Time-stamped arrival and departure logs.
- Before-and-after photos of serviced areas.
- The weather data that triggered the response.
- Product type and application rate for each [salting](/salting) pass.
- GPS tracks showing which zones were actually serviced.

If your contractor provides this automatically — not on request — you have a defensible
record without lifting a finger.

## 6. Questions to ask your contractor about the SLA

Before you sign, get straight answers to these. Vague responses are the warning sign:

- What are the exact trigger depths and response times, in writing?
- Is salting and pre-treatment included, or billed separately?
- What's your equipment depth — is there a backup if a machine fails mid-storm?
- How many other properties are committed for the same storm window? Where do we sit in
  priority?
- What insurance limits do you carry, and will you name us as an additional insured?
- How and when do we receive service documentation?
- Is this a **seasonal or per-event** structure? (If you're unsure which fits, the
  [comparison guide](/blog/seasonal-vs-per-event-snow-removal-contracts) helps you decide.)

## Before the first storm

Run the list top to bottom by early fall: walk the site, map priorities, write the triggers,
build the tree, set the documentation standard, and pin down the SLA. Award a **seasonal
contract (November 1 – April 30)** while good contractors still have capacity — by November
they're committed.
