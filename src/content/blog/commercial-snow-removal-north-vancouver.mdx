North Vancouver runs a different winter than the rest of Metro Vancouver. A lot in
Downtown Vancouver might see wet snow melt off by mid-morning; the same storm drops
heavier accumulation on Upper Lonsdale, holds it longer, and refreezes it overnight.
For anyone responsible for a retail centre, office park, or strata complex here, the
job isn't "plow when it snows" — it's running a program that stays ahead of a
freeze-thaw climate on steep ground. This is how a professional
[commercial snow removal](/snow-removal) operation actually works on the North Shore.

## Why elevation changes the whole plan

The distance between sea level and 400 metres does more to your winter than the calendar
does. Every ~100 metres of elevation gain runs the air roughly 0.6°C colder, so a rain
event on the waterfront in Lower Lonsdale is often wet snow in Lynn Valley
and packing snow above Edgemont. Higher, colder ground also means snow lingers through
the day instead of melting, then refreezes hard after dark.

Terrain compounds it. Hillside lots, sloped access roads, and multi-level parkade ramps
don't clear like a flat rectangle in Richmond — they need traction management, careful
push directions so melt doesn't sheet back across the drive lane, and heavier de-icing on
the grades. A contractor who mostly works flat suburban lots will underestimate a North
Shore site every time. Elevation is also why one quote can't cover
[North Vancouver](/north-vancouver) and West Vancouver with the same
number — snowfall exposure genuinely differs street to street.

## What actually triggers a dispatch

Reactive snow management — waiting for a complaint — is where properties get hurt, both
literally and legally. A professional program dispatches on defined triggers, not phone
calls:

- **Snowfall trigger:** crews roll when accumulation reaches the contracted depth (commonly
  2 to 3 cm), not after it's piled up.
- **Freezing trigger:** [de-icing](/de-icing) is dispatched at **0°C** whenever there's
  moisture on the ground — including clear days with no snow, when afternoon meltwater
  crosses a walkway and freezes by evening rush.
- **Pre-treatment:** anti-icing goes down *ahead* of forecast snow or freezing rain so the
  first flakes never bond to the pavement.

The mechanism that makes this reliable is automatic, weather-triggered dispatch. Instead of
a dispatcher guessing, the system watches forecast and pavement-temperature logic for each
site and sends crews before conditions turn — which is exactly why a good contractor
sometimes salts on an evening that "doesn't look icy."

## Response tiers: what "response time" really means

"Response time" is the clock from when your trigger depth is reached to when crews are
on-site. It should be written into the contract, and it should be tiered by how the
property is used:

| Site type | Typical requirement |
|---|---|
| Medical, senior living, 24/7 access | Clear and bare well before opening; tightest response |
| Retail with morning foot traffic | On-site early; entrances and accessible stalls first |
| Office / general commercial | Cleared before the morning arrival window |
| Warehouse / late-open industrial | Cleared before the operating shift |

Priority within a site matters as much as the site-level tier. Building entrances,
accessible parking, ramps, and the main pedestrian desire lines get cleared and salted
first, because that's where both the traffic and the [slip-and-fall
liability](/blog/slip-and-fall-liability-commercial-property-bc) concentrate.

## What a commercial contract actually includes

A real snow management contract is more than a truck with a plow. Expect all of these in
scope, in writing:

- **Plowing and clearing** of lots, drive lanes, loading docks, and access roads.
- **Walkway and stair clearing** by hand crews — the highest-liability zone on most sites.
- **Salting and de-icing**, both pre-treatment and post-clearing, matched to
  pavement temperature.
- **24/7 monitoring** through the season so dispatch is proactive.
- **Documentation:** time-stamped GPS and photo-verified records of every visit.

That last item is the one owners underrate. In BC, occupiers' liability turns on whether
you took *reasonable care* — and a defensible record of what was done, and when, is what
that argument is built on. GPS tracks show which areas were serviced; before-and-after
photos with timestamps show the condition. It's risk management you can actually produce
in a claim.

## The equipment behind a clear lot

Scope drives the fleet. Large lots run truck-mounted plows and skid-steer loaders;
tight corners, courtyards, and walkways need blowers and hand crews; grades and ramps
need calibrated spreaders so the salting rate matches the slope instead of
over-applying. Equipment *redundancy* is the question most owners forget to ask: if a plow
goes down at 3 a.m. mid-storm, is there a backup, or does your site wait? On the North
Shore, where a single system can run for days, depth of fleet is the difference between a
serviced lot and an excuse.

## Seasonal or per-event — and when to plan

Most North Shore commercial properties run a **seasonal contract** covering **November 1
through April 30**, because it delivers budget certainty and aligns incentives: the
contractor is paid the same whether the season brings 5 events or 30, so pre-treatment
and proactive runs are in everyone's interest. Per-event pricing can win in a light year
but blows budgets in a heavy one and quietly discourages the pre-treatment that prevents
ice. We break the trade-off down in the
[seasonal vs per-event guide](/blog/seasonal-vs-per-event-snow-removal-contracts), and
what actually drives the number is covered in our
[cost guide](/blog/snow-removal-cost-vancouver).

The best contractors fill their rosters in the fall and are effectively committed by
November. Start the conversation in September or October so there's time for a proper site
assessment — including a **satellite measurement** of your surfaces, which is how quotes
get accurate without a slow, in-person walk-around. Wait for the first snowfall and you're
choosing from whoever's left.
