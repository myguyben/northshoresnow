The most expensive salting mistake isn't using too much salt — it's salting **too late**.
Once ice has bonded to pavement, salt has to melt its way down through the ice sheet
before it can break the bond. The same application made an hour *before* the freeze
prevents the bond from ever forming, at a fraction of the material.

## The 0°C rule

For Lower Mainland commercial properties, the practical trigger is simple: **treat when
pavement temperature is heading to 0°C with any moisture present.** That includes:

- Rain ending in the evening with clearing skies (classic radiative refreeze)
- Daytime melt running across lots and refreezing in shaded areas after 3pm
- Fog or high humidity with falling overnight temperatures
- Any snowfall, before and after clearing

Notice that two of those four scenarios involve **no snow at all**. On the North Shore's
freeze-thaw cycle, most slip-and-fall incidents happen on days when it never snowed —
meltwater crossed a walkway during the afternoon and froze by evening rush.

## Air temperature lies — pavement temperature doesn't

Weather apps report air temperature at two metres. Pavement often runs 2–4 degrees colder
on clear nights because it radiates heat to the sky. When the forecast says 2°C overnight,
shaded pavement, bridge decks and parkade ramps may already be at freezing. Professional
operations treat on pavement-temperature and dew-point logic, which is why a good
contractor sometimes salts on evenings that "don't look icy."

## How much salt does a lot actually need?

Typical effective rates for rock salt on commercial lots run in the range of a few hundred
pounds per acre — far less than most self-service efforts apply. Over-salting wastes
budget, tracks into buildings, damages concrete and landscaping, and it doesn't melt ice
any faster. Calibrated spreaders plus the right timing beat "more salt" every time. Below
about −10°C, rock salt slows dramatically; that's when blended or treated products matter.

## Timing beats reaction: what a professional program looks like

A properly run winter program salts on **forecast triggers**, not on complaints:

1. **Pre-treatment** ahead of forecast snow or freezing rain, so the first flakes don't bond
2. **Post-clearing treatment** after every plow or shovel visit
3. **Freeze-event runs** whenever temperatures reach 0°C — even on clear days
4. **Documented application** with time-stamped records, because liability defence lives on paper

That last point matters more than most owners realize. In BC, occupiers' liability turns
on whether you took *reasonable care*. A documented salting program executed on
temperature triggers is exactly the evidence adjudicators look for — see our guide to
