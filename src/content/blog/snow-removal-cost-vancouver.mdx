"How much does snow removal cost?" is the first question every property manager and strata
council asks, and the honest answer is: it depends on your site, more than on the market.
A small townhouse strata and a large retail lot with 400 metres of walkway are different
jobs, and no rate sheet can price them the same. What *can* be pinned down is **what drives
the number** — so you can read a quote, compare proposals fairly, and budget with
confidence.

## Two pricing models, two risk profiles

Nearly every commercial [snow removal](/snow-removal) quote is built on one of two models.

**Seasonal contract (fixed).** You pay a set fee across the season — typically **October 1
through April 30** — covering all snow and ice service regardless of how many events hit.
Its value is budget certainty and *aligned incentives*: the contractor is paid the same for
5 events or 30, so pre-treatment and proactive runs work in everyone's favour, and seasonal
clients usually get priority dispatch.

**Per-event (pay-as-you-go).** You pay per visit, often tiered by accumulation depth. It can
win in a mild year — but a heavy North Shore winter can multiply the bill several times
over, it quietly discourages the pre-treatment that prevents ice, and per-event clients tend
to be served *after* seasonal contracts during a major storm. We compare the two in depth in
the [seasonal vs per-event guide](/blog/seasonal-vs-per-event-snow-removal-contracts).

Relatively speaking: a small strata sits at the low end of any contractor's range, a large
retail or medical property with heavy pedestrian areas at the high end, with most office and
mixed commercial sites in between. Where *you* land depends on the drivers below.

## What actually drives your number

A quote is really a bundle of these factors. The more of them tilt "up," the higher the
price — and understanding them is how you tell a fair quote from a bad one.

| Driver | Pushes cost down | Pushes cost up |
|---|---|---|
| Paved area | Small, simple lot | Large lot or multi-level parkade |
| Layout | Open rectangle, easy push | Tight corners, islands, speed bumps |
| Walkways | Few linear metres | Extensive walkway and stair clearing |
| Elevation / snowfall exposure | Near sea level | Hillside sites in [Lynn Valley](/lynn-valley), upper North Vancouver |
| Terrain | Flat | Sloped drives, ramps needing extra traction |
| Response window | Opens mid-morning | Must be bare before 6 a.m. |
| Access | Open overnight, room to stack snow | Narrow access, overnight-parked cars, nowhere to pile snow |

Two of these deserve emphasis on the North Shore. **Elevation and snowfall exposure** aren't
cosmetic — a property up the hill genuinely sees more events and colder temperatures, so it
needs more visits and more [de-icing](/de-icing) material across the season. And **service
level** — how tight your response window and trigger depth are — scales cost directly,
because bare-pavement-by-6-a.m. requires more resources and more frequent runs than a lot
that opens at nine.

## Why satellite measurement makes a quote accurate

The old way to price a site was a slow in-person walk-around with a wheel and a clipboard —
or worse, an eyeball estimate that padded the number to cover the guesswork. We measure
surfaces from **satellite imagery** instead: the actual square metres of lot, the linear
metres of walkway, the ramps and islands, mapped precisely before anyone quotes.

That does two things. It makes the quote *accurate* — you're billed for the surfaces you
actually have, not a rounded-up guess — and it makes proposals *comparable*, because the
scope is measured rather than assumed. It also means a fast turnaround: we can often price a
property without waiting for a site visit to line up.

## How to compare proposals fairly

The cheapest quote is frequently the most expensive one once the season runs. Before
comparing prices, confirm you're comparing the same *job*:

1. **Scope** — does it include plowing, [salting](/salting), walkway clearing, and
   monitoring, or are some billed separately?
2. **Trigger depth** — a 5 cm trigger is a very different service than 2.5 cm.
3. **Response time** — how fast crews arrive after the trigger.
4. **Pre-treatment** — included, or an add-on? It's one of the most valuable line items.
5. **Documentation** — time-stamped logs and photos, which double as your
   [liability protection](/blog/slip-and-fall-liability-commercial-property-bc).
6. **Insurance limits** and whether your property can be named as additional insured.
7. **Equipment depth** — is there backup if a machine fails mid-storm?

Watch for the tricks that make a low base price misleading: salt billed as a per-visit
surcharge, overnight or holiday premium rates (exactly when you need service), and minimum
call-out fees that make light events disproportionately expensive.

## Budgeting for the season

- **Quote early** — request proposals in September or October so you can compare and budget
  before the first storm.
- **Budget the seasonal figure** if you go fixed; it's the number multiplied by the contract
  months, and it won't move.
- **Add a 10–15% contingency** for extras a heavy season can bring, like snow hauling after
  an exceptional event.
- **Frame it as risk management.** A single serious slip-and-fall claim can cost more than
  many seasons of service. The invoice isn't just maintenance — it's the exposure you're
  buying down.
