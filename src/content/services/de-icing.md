## Anti-icing beats de-icing every time

There are two ways to fight ice, and they aren't equal. Anti-icing is proactive: treating a
surface before the storm so ice can't bond to the pavement in the first place. De-icing is
reactive: breaking down ice that has already formed and frozen to the ground. Anti-icing
wins almost every time — it uses less material, works faster, and keeps the surface safer
from the first flake. North Shore Snow leads with anti-icing wherever the forecast allows,
then follows up with de-icing when a storm outpaces the pre-treatment.

## Dispatched automatically the moment it hits 0°C

Ice doesn't wait for a phone call, so neither do we. Our crews are dispatched on automatic
weather triggers, and de-icing runs whenever temperatures reach 0°C — even with no snow in
the forecast. A clear, wet lot at plus-two in the afternoon becomes black ice at minus-one
overnight; automatic dispatch is what gets salt down before that flip happens, instead of
after someone slips. It's the same 24/7 monitoring that drives our
[snow plowing routes](/snow-plowing).

## Liquid brine or granular — the right tool for the temperature

Pre-storm, we apply liquid brine: a salt solution that coats the pavement and stops ice
from anchoring, so the next plow scrapes down to bare ground. Granular salt and treated
blends go down on active ice and refreeze, where you need melting power on contact. Standard
rock salt melts reliably down to about -12°C; below that we switch to treated and blended
materials that keep working in deeper cold. Choosing between them per surface and per
temperature is the difference between a lot that stays clear and one that just looks
treated. Our sister service, [calibrated salting and ice melt](/salting), covers the
granular side in detail.

## Black ice and the North Shore freeze-thaw

The North Shore's elevation gradient makes ice management its own discipline. A property in
[West Vancouver](/west-vancouver) can sit above freezing while a site up in
[Lynn Valley](/lynn-valley) is already glazing over. Daytime melt runs across lots and
walkways, then refreezes into black ice the moment the sun drops — invisible, and exactly
where people walk from the car to the door. Repeated freeze-thaw cycles reload that risk
every single night, which is why de-icing on the North Shore is a season-long routine, not a
one-time storm response.

## The liability case writes itself

— and "we usually salt" is not a defence. A timestamped, GPS-stamped record showing your
entrances were treated at 5:40am is. Every de-icing visit we run files exactly that proof to
